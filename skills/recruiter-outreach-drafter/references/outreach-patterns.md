# Outreach Patterns

Six message types, each with structure, two example messages, common pitfalls, and tone notes. Pair with `channel-conventions.md` for length calibration.

The skill picks one type based on the user's inputs, then drafts two variants within that type using different hooks or asks. The example messages below are anonymized illustrations — the skill draws real specifics from `profile.md` and the JD.

---

## 1. Cold to recruiter / hiring manager

**When to use:** you're reaching out cold to someone at a target company who hasn't spoken with you before. Typically about a specific role.

**Structure:**

- Hook: a specific signal (the JD, a recent project, a post by them or a colleague)
- Context: one line on you + why you're reaching out
- Ask: a small, specific request (15-min chat / question about the role / pointer to the right person)

**Example A — LinkedIn DM to a recruiter (90 words):**

> Hi [Recruiter name] — I saw the [Role] role at [Company] and the [specific JD detail] stood out. I'm a [your role] at [your context] running a similar [implementation / program], and I think the role's a clean fit for the kind of work I want to spend the next chapter on.
>
> Would you be open to a 15-min conversation next week? Happy to share my resume ahead of time if useful.

**Example B — Email to a hiring manager (130 words):**

> **Subject:** [Role] — quick intro
>
> Hi [Manager name],
>
> I noticed the [Role] role on the [Company] careers page, and the [specific JD detail] line up with what I've been spending most of my time on. I'm currently leading a [relevant project], and have been working on [a relevant capability].
>
> I'd welcome 15 minutes to walk through fit and answer anything on the [relevant] background. Happy to send a resume in advance, or just talk first — whichever works.
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

**Pitfalls:**

- **Generic hook.** "I'm interested in your company" is not a hook; it's filler.
- **Credential dump.** The context line is one sentence, not a paragraph.
- **Ambiguous ask.** "Open to chat sometime?" gives them nothing to say yes to. Name a duration.

**Tone:** confident peer, not deferential applicant. Direct but warm.

## 2. Cold to a peer practitioner in your field

**When to use:** you're reaching out to another practitioner in your field at a target company — not necessarily because there's a role, sometimes just to compare notes or build a relationship for later.

**Structure:**

- Hook: a field-specific angle (their tooling stack, a podcast appearance, a philosophy post, an operating principle they've published)
- Context: one line on you + why you're reaching out
- Ask: a chat, a question, advice, or a specific topic to compare notes on

**Example A — LinkedIn DM (95 words):**

> Hi [Name] — saw your post on [a specific topic, e.g. a platform migration] and the [specific detail] you ran. I'm in the middle of something similar right now and made some of the same calls. Would love to hear what you'd do differently in hindsight.
>
> Worth 20 minutes sometime in the next two weeks? Happy to share what I've been learning too.

**Example B — async tool (Slack/Teams/etc.) (80 words):**

> Hey [Name] — your team's site mentions you're building out the function deliberately rather than scaling staff with headcount. I'd be curious how you're thinking about that — I've made similar design choices on a lean team and it's been some of the most interesting work I've done.
>
> Worth a 20-min comparison-of-notes? No agenda beyond that.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Pitchy.** Peer outreach isn't a soft job application. Don't lead with "I'm exploring opportunities."
- **Asking for too much.** "Worth chatting?" with no topic is harder to say yes to than "Worth a 20-min comparison on [specific topic]?"

**Tone:** peer-to-peer, curious, specific.

## 3. Warm intro / referral

**When to use:** there's a connector (a former colleague, a past-organization contact, a mutual on LinkedIn) who has agreed to refer you or who you're asking to refer you.

**Structure:**

- Hook: the connector's name and the connection's context (one line)
- Context: one line on you + the specific role / interest
- Ask: a clear request — the referral itself, or a chat that leads to it

**Example A — Email to a connector asking for the referral (110 words):**

> **Subject:** Referral ask — [Company] [Role] role
>
> Hi [Connector name],
>
> Hope you've been well since [shared context]. I'm currently [your role] and looking at the next chapter — saw [Company] has an open [Role] role that maps cleanly to my background ([2–3 specific matches]).
>
> Would you be open to passing my name along to whoever's running hiring there? Happy to send my resume and a short note you could forward, or to chat first if you want context. I know referral asks aren't trivial — no pressure if it's not a fit right now.
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

**Example B — LinkedIn DM to the contact when the connector has already agreed (75 words):**

> Hi [Contact name] — [connector] suggested I reach out. We [shared context], and they've been following the work I've been doing ([one-line scope]).
>
> They mentioned the [Role] role might be a good fit. Would you be open to a 20-min conversation about it?

**Pitfalls:**

- **Treating the connector as a guaranteed yes.** Always give them an out. "No pressure if it's not a fit right now" earns more goodwill than presumptuous framing.
- **Asking for the referral and the chat in one message.** Pick one ask per message. The chat usually comes after the referral, not alongside.

**Tone:** warm, respectful of the connector's social capital.

## 4. Role-specific interest after applying

**When to use:** you've applied via the careers page and want to follow up directly with a recruiter or hiring manager to add context and signal interest.

**Structure:**

- Hook: reference the application + the specific role
- Context: one or two specific points the resume can't carry (a connection to a recent company move, a specific match)
- Ask: a request for a conversation or for the application to be flagged to the right person

**Example A — Email (115 words):**

> **Subject:** Application — [Role]
>
> Hi [Recruiter name],
>
> I applied via the careers page earlier this week for the [Role] role, and wanted to add a bit of context that the resume can't fully carry.
>
> Two things stood out to me about the role: [JD detail #1] (where I've [relevant experience]), and [JD detail #2] (where I've [relevant experience]).
>
> Would you be open to a 15-min conversation?
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

**Example B — LinkedIn DM to the hiring manager (95 words):**

> Hi [Manager name] — I applied for the [Role] role earlier this week. Two things I wanted to flag directly that don't fit cleanly on a resume:
>
> 1. [A current project that mirrors what the role is built around.]
> 2. [A capability that matches a key project named in the JD.]
>
> Worth a 15-min conversation?

**Pitfalls:**

- **Restating the resume.** This message adds context the resume can't carry. If the message is just a paraphrase of the resume, skip it.
- **Multiple asks.** One ask per message.

**Tone:** professional, specific, confident without being pushy.

## 5. General networking

**When to use:** you're reaching out without a specific role in mind — building a relationship for later, or genuinely curious about someone's work.

**Structure:**

- Hook: a topical interest, a recent post, a project at their company, a shared background
- Context: one line on what you're up to currently
- Ask: a low-friction chat or an exchange of notes — not a job-related ask

**Example A — LinkedIn DM (100 words):**

> Hi [Name] — your recent post on [specific topic] was the most useful thing I've read this month. I run a [relevant system] that ended up making similar calls and the part you wrote about [specific detail] hit hard.
>
> Not reaching out about anything specific — just wanted to compare notes if you're open to a 20-min conversation in the next month or so. No agenda beyond your post.

**Example B — async tool (Slack/Teams/etc.) (75 words):**

> Hey [Name] — saw that you've been doing [your field] consulting. I'm in the same field and increasingly thinking about consulting alongside a main role.
>
> Would love to compare notes if you're open to it — async or 30 min sync, whichever is easier. No agenda.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Hidden job ask.** Networking with a stealth job-search agenda usually reads as exactly that. If you want a job conversation, use type 1, 2, or 4. Don't bait-and-switch.
- **Vague.** "Would love to connect" with no topic is harder to act on than a specific compare-notes ask.

**Tone:** curious peer, no agenda.

## 6. Follow-up after a touchpoint

**When to use:** you've had a conversation, an interview, an email exchange — and want to follow up to advance the relationship or close out a loop.

**Structure:**

- Hook: reference the specific moment (the conversation, what they said)
- Context: what's changed since, or what you wanted to add
- Ask: clear next step (next round, a confirmation, a specific question)

**Example A — Email after a hiring manager round (110 words):**

> **Subject:** Following up — [Role]
>
> Hi [Manager name],
>
> Thanks for the time yesterday. The part of the conversation that stuck with me most was [a specific thing they walked through] — specifically the [specific trade-off] you're weighing. After we spoke, I dug into a few related patterns from my own work and I think the scope you described is exactly the seat I'd want to be in.
>
> Two questions if useful: (1) what's the timeline for next steps, and (2) is there a follow-up artifact or take-home you'd want me to send ahead?
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

**Example B — Slack after a casual chat (60 words):**

> Hey [Name] — really enjoyed the conversation last week, especially your read on [a specific point]. I keep thinking about that framing.
>
> Quick follow-up: you mentioned [specific contact] was the right person to talk to about [topic]. Would you be open to making the intro?

**Pitfalls:**

- **Generic thanks.** "Thanks for your time" is filler; specific gratitude ("that conversation about X was the most useful 30 minutes of my week") earns the airtime.
- **Two asks.** Pick one ask per follow-up. Save the other for the next round.
- **Too long.** Follow-ups are shorter than the initial message, not longer.

**Tone:** warm, specific, decisive about the next step.

---

## Cross-pattern rules

- **One type per message.** Don't mix cold-to-recruiter with general-networking. Pick the type and commit.
- **Hook is always specific.** Across all six types, the hook must reference something concrete you actually know. If you can't find one, the message isn't ready to send.
- **Two variants use different hooks or different asks** — not the same message with synonyms.
- **Subject lines for emails are tactical.** Subject = "[Role] application" or "Referral ask — [Role]" is good. Subject = "Hello" or "Quick question" is not.
