# Channel Conventions

Length, tone, salutation, and signature norms per channel. The skill uses this to calibrate the draft to the medium — a 200-word LinkedIn DM reads as bloated; a 40-word cold email reads as a typo.

---

## LinkedIn DM

**Target length:** 60–120 words.

**Tone:** conversational, peer-to-peer. Direct but not abrupt.

**Salutation:** "Hi [first name]" or "Hey [first name]." Drop the "Hi" entirely if the relationship is casual. Never "Dear" — it reads as a cold email pretending to be a DM.

**Signature:** none. The platform shows your name and headline already.

**What works:**

- Specific hooks (a post, a project, a JD detail)
- Low-friction asks (15-min chat, a pointer to the right person)
- One paragraph or two short ones, never three

**What doesn't:**

- Formal salutations
- Email-style signatures
- Long credential dumps
- More than 150 words

**Special case — connection request note:**

LinkedIn now caps connection-request notes at ~300 characters. Use them for one-line context only ("[your role], would love to compare notes on [shared topic]"). The actual outreach message goes in a DM after the connection is accepted.

---

## LinkedIn InMail

**Target length:** 100–150 words.

**Tone:** slightly more formal than a DM. Subject line is required.

**Salutation:** "Hi [first name]" or "Hi [first name] [last name]" for senior contacts.

**Signature:** one line at the end — "Best, [your first name]" or similar.

**What works:**

- A subject line that earns the open ("[field] [region] role — quick intro")
- A clearer ask than a DM (since the recipient pays attention cost on InMails)
- Slightly more credential context than a DM (since they may not know you)

**What doesn't:**

- Generic subject lines ("Hello," "Quick question")
- Selling at first contact

**Subject line conventions:**

- Specific over generic: "[field] — [your location], [N] years remote-first" beats "Hello"
- Reference the role if applying: "Application — [Role title]"
- Reference a connection if warm: "[connector name] suggested I reach out"

## Email (cold)

**Target length:** 100–180 words.

**Tone:** professional, specific. Slightly more formal than LinkedIn.

**Salutation:** "Hi [first name]" or "Hello [first name]." Never "Dear" unless the role demands extreme formality (rare).

**Signature:** your email signature with name, current role (or "[Your field] consultant" if consulting context), and contact info. Don't pad with quotes or fancy formatting.

**What works:**

- Subject line that is doing real work
- A specific hook in the first sentence
- One ask, clearly stated
- A line about next steps ("Happy to send my resume ahead of time")

**What doesn't:**

- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I am writing to express my interest"
- "I came across your profile / your company"
- More than 200 words

**Subject line conventions:**

- For applications: "Application — [Role title], [your name]"
- For role inquiries: "[Role] inquiry — [your name]"
- For referrals: "Referral ask — [Role] at [Company]"
- For general networking: keep it specific to the topic, not generic

---

## Email (warm)

**Target length:** 80–150 words.

**Tone:** warmer than cold email. Can reference shared history or the connector.

**Salutation:** "Hi [first name]" or "Hey [first name]" if the relationship is casual.

**Signature:** standard email signature; informal sign-off ("Best," "Cheers," "Talk soon").

**What works:**

- Personal context that wouldn't make sense in a cold message
- Slightly more compressed than cold (since the relationship is already established)
- Direct asks ("Worth a 20-min chat?")

**What doesn't:**

- Over-formalizing a warm relationship
- Pretending the relationship is colder than it is

---

## Async messaging tool (Slack / Teams / etc.)

**Target length:** 60–100 words.

**Tone:** async-native. Slightly more substantial than a quick chat message but lighter than an email.

**Salutation:** "Hey [first name]" or just the name with no salutation. Async-tool culture varies but tends informal.

**Signature:** none.

**What works:**

- Specificity (async-tool users tend to value concrete signal and no filler)
- Slightly longer than a quick chat message but still tight
- A thread starter that earns its own thread title if posted publicly

**What doesn't:**

- Formal salutations
- Email-length messages
- Selling

---

## Slack

**Target length:** 40–80 words.

**Tone:** conversational, fast. Often the same channel where regular work happens.

**Salutation:** "Hey [first name]" or just `@[firstname]` mention.

**Signature:** none.

**What works:**

- Extreme concision
- A single ask, no preamble
- A follow-up-friendly framing (Slack messages get lost; make it easy to respond to)

**What doesn't:**

- More than 80 words
- Anything that should be an email pretending to be a Slack message
- Cold outreach to people you don't share a Slack with (this is what LinkedIn / email is for)

## Channel-selection heuristic

When the channel isn't specified, use this rough priority:

1. **Email** — when there's a clear professional context and a specific ask
2. **LinkedIn DM** — for cold or semi-warm professional outreach without an email address
3. **LinkedIn InMail** — only if you have InMail credits and no other connection route exists (most outreach is better as a connection request + DM)
4. **Async tool (Slack / Teams / etc.)** — only for contacts already in those channels with you

If the contact is a recruiter actively running a search, prefer email (recruiters live in inbox). If the contact is a peer in your field, LinkedIn DM is usually warmer.

---

## Signature reference

For email, a working signature looks like:

> Best,
> [Your Name]
> [Your title / discipline] | [Current role or company]
> [your email] | [your phone]
> [your LinkedIn URL]

For consulting context, swap "[Your title / discipline] | [Current role]" for "[Your field] Consulting." Don't include the signature in LinkedIn DMs or async-tool messages (Slack/Teams/etc.) — those channels surface the identity already.

---

## Cross-channel rules

- **Hook specificity is the same standard across all channels.** A specific hook works in 60 words on Slack and in 150 words on email. A generic hook fails in both.
- **Forbidden-phrase list is the same across all channels.** "Wanted to reach out" reads the same way on LinkedIn as it does on email.
- **No emoji unless the channel pattern with this contact includes them.** Cold outreach: no. Peer LinkedIn DM with established rapport: occasional, sparing.
- **Don't paste the email message verbatim into LinkedIn.** Calibrate to the channel — what works in 150 words on email needs to compress to 80 on LinkedIn.
