# Counter-Script Archetypes

Script patterns by lever, in your voice (calibrated from your `profile.md`). Each archetype includes a sample script, what to expect them to say back, and follow-up moves.

Voice rules: warm-but-direct, principled framing, no aggressive anchoring, no apologetic hedging. Same forbidden-phrase list as the rest of the skill set.

---

## Base — anchored, principled

**When to use:** the base in the offer is below market or below your target, and the reasoning is clear (scope, experience, market data).

**Sample script:**

> "Thanks again for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. Based on what I'm seeing in the market for roles at this level and geography, and the scope we discussed ([name the specific scope: e.g. multi-region rollout, platform implementation, process automation]), I was hoping we could get to €X on base. Is there flexibility there?"

**Why this works:**

- Opens with a real positive ("genuinely excited") so the counter doesn't read as transactional
- Anchors the ask in market data + scope, not personal need
- Names a specific number, not a vague "higher"
- Ends with an invitation ("Is there flexibility?") rather than an ultimatum

**Expected responses:**

- **"That's outside our range."** → "Understood. Can you share the band you're working with? I want to make sure we're in the same ZOPA."
- **"We can do part of that — maybe €Y."** → "Thanks for the move. Let me think through that with the rest of the package — specifically the equity and the work-from-anywhere piece — and come back."
- **"Let me check with my manager."** → "Of course. Take your time."

## Base — direct

**When to use:** when the relationship is established enough to be direct, or when the principled version has already been deployed and didn't land.

**Sample script:**

> "I appreciate the offer. To be straightforward — €X is the number that makes this work for me, given the role scope and where the market is. If we can land there or close, I'm ready to sign. What's possible on your side?"

**Why this works:**

- Direct without being aggressive
- Names the number and the reasoning
- Signals readiness to close, which lowers their negotiation pressure
- Asks them to engage rather than ending the conversation

**Use sparingly.** The principled version should be the first move; the direct version is for when more directness is warranted.

## Equity

**When to use:** the equity grant is the lever to negotiate, either because base is fixed or because the equity is the load-bearing part of the comp.

**Sample script:**

> "The equity grant is interesting and I'd want to make sure I understand it properly. A few questions on that side: what's the most recent 409A or preferred price you'd use to value the grant? What's the vesting structure — standard four-year with one-year cliff, or something different? And is acceleration on change-of-control part of the standard package?
>
> Depending on those answers, I'd want to discuss whether we can get the grant to [Y shares / $Z notional value], given the role's scope and the company's stage."

**Why this works:**

- Asks the diligence questions first, anchoring the conversation in real numbers
- Names the specific terms (409A, vesting, acceleration) so the counterpart knows you're informed
- Frames the counter-ask conditionally ("depending on those answers") rather than demanding upfront

**Expected responses:**

- **They share the diligence info** → use it to compute the real comp value, then return with a specific equity ask
- **"That's the standard grant we offer at this level."** → "Understood. Given the role's scope spans [specific elements], I'd want to push for [specific increase]. Is that something we can explore?"
- **"We can't move on equity."** → "Understood. Let me think about whether the rest of the package gets us there."

## Work-from-anywhere

**When to use:** essentially always, unless the offer already has written work-from-anywhere terms that accommodate any recurring stays abroad in your profile.

**Sample script:**

> "One thing I want to be upfront about — I spend [N weeks] in [month(s)] in [country] each year for [reason]. I work fully remotely during those weeks; the time difference works with my schedule. I'd want this in writing as part of the offer. Is that something we can include?"

**Why this works:**

- Direct and clear — no hedging
- Frames it as a known commitment, not a request
- Names the specific times and duration
- Asks for written confirmation, which signals seriousness

**Expected responses:**

- **"Yes, that's fine, we'll include it in the offer."** → "Thank you. Looking forward to seeing the updated terms."
- **"We don't typically formalize this kind of thing."** → "I understand it's not standard, but for me this is non-negotiable, so I'd want it on paper. Can we work that out?"
- **"We're a hybrid role — does this work with the in-office cadence?"** → "Let's talk through that. What's the actual cadence expected, and how does it flex around those weeks?"
- **"No, that doesn't work for us."** → (See "What if they push back hard" below.)

## Signing bonus (bridge a gap)

**When to use:** the base can't move enough but you want to close the gap, or to compensate for a delayed start date, or to offset something you're leaving behind in your current role.

**Sample script:**

> "If base is harder to move on, would a signing bonus of €X help bridge the gap? That would let me feel comfortable with the overall package without putting more pressure on the base number."

**Why this works:**

- Frames the signing bonus as a solution for both sides, not an extraction
- Names a specific number
- Signals willingness to close ("comfortable with the overall package")

**Variations:**

- Pair with a vesting schedule on the signing bonus (e.g., "vested over 12 months with full clawback if I leave") to make it easier for them to say yes
- Use as the final close lever when base and equity are settled

## Multi-issue package

**When to use:** when individual-lever negotiation has stalled or when packaging the asks together is more efficient.

**Sample script:**

> "Let me share what would make this an easy yes for me. If we can land at €X on base, get the work-from-anywhere written into the offer, and add [€Y signing bonus / Z equity / extra PTO days], I'd be ready to sign. How does that land on your side?"

**Why this works:**

- Names the full package, not piecemeal
- Frames it as a closing move ("ready to sign"), which lowers their friction
- Asks for their reaction without committing to a position they have to defend
- "Easy yes" framing invites collaboration

**This is the most powerful script in the set.** Use when ready to close.

## What if they push back hard

When the company says "we can't do that" on a major lever, the options are:

### Option 1 — Reframe and ask why

> "Help me understand the constraint. Is it a budget ceiling, a policy thing, or something else? I'd rather work the actual constraint than guess."

This is principled curiosity. Often reveals which lever they *can* move on.

### Option 2 — Trade

> "If base is fixed at €Y, would there be room to move on [equity / work-from-anywhere / signing bonus / PTO] to get us closer to where I'd need to be?"

This invites the multi-issue trade.

### Option 3 — Hold and walk away (for non-negotiables only)

> "I appreciate that. For me, the work-from-anywhere piece isn't something I can be flexible on — it's a hard constraint for me [state the reason briefly]. If that's not workable on your side, I understand, but I think we'd need to revisit whether this role is the right fit."

**Use only for true non-negotiables.** Walking is real, not a tactic.

## What if they ask "what's the most you've earned" or "what's your current comp"

These are anchor-setting questions designed to get you to anchor low. The deflection:

> "I'd rather focus on what the right comp looks like for this specific role and scope, rather than anchor on what I've earned previously — different role, different context. Based on the market for roles at this level and geography and the scope we discussed, the range I'm working with is €X–Y."

This reframes the conversation onto the role, not your history.

If pushed harder, share the band but not a single number:

> "I'm currently in a band around €X–Y, but I don't think it's the most useful anchor — the scope here is different. Let's focus on what this role should pay."

---

## What if they ask for a single number

Don't give one. Give a range:

> "I don't want to anchor on a single number too early — let me share the range I'm working with: €X–Y, with the bottom of that range above where I'd feel good and the top being where I'd say 'easy yes.' What's the band you're working with on your side?"

If forced to commit to a number, anchor at the top of the range (the aspirational), not the target.

---

## Closing — accepting the offer

When the negotiation is done and you're ready to accept:

> "Thanks for working through this with me. The package as it stands works — let me sleep on it one more night to make sure I'm landing the right way, and I'll come back tomorrow with a yes."

**Always sleep on it.** Even when ready to accept.

## Voice notes on counter scripts

These scripts are starter templates. The skill should adapt them to your specific voice patterns (from `profile.md`):

- **Warm-but-direct opener.** "Thanks again for the offer" or "I appreciate the offer" — never "I am thrilled to receive the offer."
- **Principled framing.** Anchor the ask on market / scope / experience — never on personal need.
- **No apologetic hedging.** "I'm hoping" is fine; "Sorry to ask" is not.
- **Specific over generic.** Name the lever, the number, the reason.
- **End with an invitation.** "Is there flexibility?" / "How does that land?" / "What's possible on your side?" — invitations are stronger than ultimatums.

Run the same forbidden-phrase check as the rest of the skill set:

- "Leverage" as verb → no
- "Synergy" → no
- "I am thrilled" → no
- "Hope this finds you well" → no
- "Looking forward to hearing from you" → no
