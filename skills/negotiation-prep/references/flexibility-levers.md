# Flexibility Levers

Non-cash levers worth negotiating, in rough priority order. Each lever has estimated
value, common negotiation framing, and what not to trade away. **Which levers matter
most, and which are non-negotiable, comes from your `profile.md`** ("Flexibility &
remote work requirements" and "Values" sections). The order below is a sensible default
for a remote-first candidate — re-rank it to fit your profile.

The skill surfaces these in the "Negotiation order" section when relevant — sometimes a
flexibility lever should be pulled before base, depending on the offer and your profile.

Replace the bracketed specifics in the framing scripts with your own details before
using them.

---

## 1. Work-from-anywhere policy (often non-negotiable)

**The need:** if your profile flags recurring stays abroad (e.g. several weeks a year
working remotely from another country for family or other reasons), this is usually the
highest-value lever and may be non-negotiable.

**Value:** can be worth tens of thousands over a few years. An offer that blocks it is
materially worse than the comp number suggests. An offer that supports it explicitly in
writing is materially better.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "One thing I want to be upfront about — I spend [N weeks] in [month(s)] in [country]
> each year for [reason]. I work fully remotely during those weeks, and the time
> difference works with my schedule. I'd want this in writing as part of the offer. Is
> that something we can include?"

**What to never trade away:** the work-from-anywhere allowance itself, if your profile
marks it non-negotiable. Trade other levers, trade base, not this.

**Red flags:**

- "We have a flexible culture" without specifics — push for written terms
- "We typically allow X but it depends on the role" — get the specific terms in writing
- "We've never had to formalize this" — formalize it as a condition

---

## 2. Async-first culture (high priority for remote-first candidates)

**The need:** async as an actual operating principle, not a stated value. Calendar
discipline that doesn't assume every collaboration is a meeting. Written-first
documentation culture.

**Value:** quality of life, focus capacity, energy for the work itself. Hard to quantify
directly but real.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "How does the team operate day-to-day in terms of sync vs. async? I work best in an
> async-first cadence — written-first, calendar lighter — and that's been a big part of
> what's made my best roles work."

**What to never trade away:** if the role is fundamentally sync-heavy (multiple daily
standups, packed calendar, "we use Slack like a chat room"), that's a real role-fit
signal even at the right comp.

**Red flags:**

- "We try to be async" alongside "but we have a daily standup at [time]"
- Time-zone overlap requirements that clash with your working hours
- All-hands every week instead of monthly or async

---

## 3. PTO generosity (medium-high priority)

**The need:** set your own target in your profile (e.g. 25+ days base, plus reasonable
flex on top).

**Value:** convertible to a cash equivalent for comparison (see
`comp-comparison-rubric.md`).

**Negotiation framing:**

> "On PTO — what's the policy, and how does it actually work in practice? I typically
> use [N]+ days a year, including any work-from-anywhere stretches."

**What to trade carefully:** unlimited PTO that culturally doesn't get used is worth
less than a fixed allotment that everyone uses. Push for clarity on what the average
team member actually takes.

**Red flags:**

- "Unlimited PTO" with a cultural expectation of 12–15 days taken
- "We have generous PTO" with no specifics
- A "minimum required" PTO policy that's actually a cap

---

## 4. Hours flexibility (medium priority)

**The need:** no rigid 9–5 expectation if that doesn't fit you. A core-hours model
(e.g. an overlap window) is usually fine; strict in-office or strict synchronous hours
is friction.

**Value:** comparable to async-first — quality of life and energy.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "How do hours work day-to-day? I run on [your rhythm — e.g. a slightly later schedule]
> and do my best work [when]. Does that fit the team's cadence?"

**What to trade:** willing to commit to core overlap hours if the rest of the day is
flexible. Decide in your profile where your hard line is.

---

## 5. Consulting / outside-work compatibility (medium priority)

**The need:** if you maintain (or want to maintain) a consulting / contractor practice
alongside the role, get clarity on whether the offer allows it.

**Value:** preserves an income stream and optionality. Supports a stated direction of
keeping consulting alive between or alongside roles.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "I maintain a selective consulting practice alongside my main role. It doesn't
> conflict, but I'd want clarity on whether the offer allows for it. Some companies
> require disclosure or approval; others have a flat 'no outside work' policy. Where
> does [Company] sit on that?"

**What to never trade away:** the right to do *any* consulting work, if that matters to
you. Specific disclosure or approval requirements are usually fine; a flat ban is
friction.

**Red flags:**

- "No outside work" clauses in the standard contract
- Non-compete clauses extending to consulting in adjacent industries
- "We'd prefer if you focused 100% on us" without flexibility

---

## 6. Title that matches scope (medium priority)

**The need:** a title that represents the actual seniority and scope of the role, for
resume signaling and future trajectory.

**Value:** matters for the next role's positioning. A senior title at a larger company
can carry more market signal than a manager title at a tiny one, even at identical scope.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "On the title — is there flexibility on how this role is named? Given the scope we
> discussed [specifics], something like [alternative title] might better match what the
> role actually is."

**What to trade:** sometimes title is flexible when comp isn't. Worth asking.

---

## 7. Sabbatical / extended-leave eligibility (low-medium priority)

**The need:** clarity on sabbatical or extended-leave policy, especially if it could
pair with extended stays abroad later.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "Does [Company] have a sabbatical policy? Not planning to take one in year one, but
> it's something I think about over a longer horizon."

---

## 8. L&D / coaching budget (low priority)

**The need:** budget for certifications, coaching, conferences.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "What's the L&D / professional development budget? I'm thinking about [specific cert
> or coaching] over the next 12 months."

---

## 9. Equipment / home office stipend (low priority)

**Value:** small but visible — companies that handle this thoughtfully signal a broader
posture toward remote-first work.

**Negotiation framing:**

> "What's the home office or equipment stipend like? Nothing major needed on my side,
> but useful to know."

---

## Lever-trading strategy

If the company can't move on base, push the flexibility levers in priority order
(re-rank for your profile):

1. **Work-from-anywhere** (always, if not already in the offer and your profile needs it)
2. **Async-first / hours flexibility** (clarify operating reality, not just stated values)
3. **PTO** (if base is fixed)
4. **Consulting compatibility** (if not addressed and relevant to you)
5. **Signing bonus** (last, smallest, often easiest to grant)

If multiple are needed, **package them**:

> "If we can't quite get to €X on base, would you be open to a combination — €Y on base,
> work-from-anywhere written into the offer, and a €Z signing bonus to bridge the gap?
> That would be an easy yes from me."

---

## What not to trade

Hold firm on whatever your profile marks non-negotiable. Common examples:

- **Work-from-anywhere** for any recurring stays abroad your profile depends on.
- **The right to do any consulting work**, if you've flagged that. Specific approval
  requirements are fine; a flat ban may be a dealbreaker.
- **Anything that would force a schedule you can't sustainably hold.** "Flexible, but
  you need to be on [a specific] schedule" isn't actually flexible.

---

## Honest read on flexibility-as-comp

For some people flexibility is a nice-to-have; for others it's genuinely comp. If your
profile flags recurring stays abroad, those alone can be worth real money in terms of
family time, life arrangement, and the things they make possible. The skill's job is to
make sure the flexibility items your profile cares about show up in the negotiation
explicitly — named, written into the offer, and treated as real levers rather than
something to "see how it goes" after starting.
