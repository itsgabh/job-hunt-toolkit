# Negotiation Framework

The core concepts and principles that shape every negotiation prep. Read this before drafting any counter — the framework determines what kind of script makes sense.

---

## Core concepts

### BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement

What you do if this offer falls through entirely. The BATNA sets the floor: any offer worse than the BATNA isn't worth taking.

Quality of BATNA matters as much as the value:

- **Strong BATNA** — staying in your current role (known role, known comp, known flexibility) is high-quality. Another finalized offer with a signed agreement is also strong.
- **Medium BATNA** — another offer in advanced stages but not signed. Active job search with multiple early-stage conversations.
- **Weak BATNA** — passive job search, no offers in flight.

A weak BATNA doesn't mean you shouldn't negotiate — it means the negotiation has to be more careful, and the walk-away has to reflect the real alternative.

### ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement

The overlap between your minimum acceptable (your walk-away) and the company's maximum offerable (their budget ceiling). If the ZOPA is non-existent — your minimum is above their ceiling — there's no deal to be made.

The skill's job is to find out where the ZOPA actually is. Often, what looks like "they don't have budget" is actually "they haven't been asked properly."

### Anchoring

The first number stated has gravity. If you state a number first, that becomes the starting point. If the company states first, their number anchors the conversation.

Two anchoring rules:

1. **If asked first ("what's your expected comp?"), don't give a single number.** Give a range with the bottom of the range above your target.
2. **If the company states first, don't accept their anchor.** Counter with reasoning, not just a different number.

### Reservation price

The walk-away. The minimum you will accept. Never disclosed to the other side — this is internal-use only.

### Aspiration price

The number that would make you say yes immediately, no further negotiation. Used sparingly — usually surfaced only when the counterpart asks "what would close this."

### Multi-issue trading

Negotiations are usually multi-variable: base, bonus, equity, PTO, work-from-anywhere, sign-on, start date, title. Single-issue negotiation (base alone) has less room for creativity than multi-issue (base + work-from-anywhere + equity).

Package the levers. "If we could get to €X on base and put work-from-anywhere into the offer in writing, I'd be ready to sign" is stronger than three separate negotiations.

## Principles

### 1. Principled framing beats aggressive framing

The goal isn't to "win" — it's to land on a deal that's right for both sides. Aggressive framing ("this is what I demand") creates resistance; principled framing ("based on [reason], I'm looking at [number]") invites collaboration.

The reasoning matters more than the number. "I'm looking at €X based on the role's scope and [a specific scope/experience point]" earns more room than "I want €X."

### 2. Salary is one variable, not the whole conversation

Depending on your profile, flexibility — work-from-anywhere for any recurring stays abroad, async culture, hours flexibility, consulting compatibility — can be worth real money. A €5K higher base with a rigid hybrid cadence is a worse offer than a €5K lower base with explicit work-from-anywhere.

Negotiate the package, not just the salary.

### 3. "What would make this an easy yes?" is a powerful question

If the company asks what number would close it, the answer is the **aspirational price**, not the target. The aspirational is the number that makes you actually want to say yes.

If you ask the company "what would make this an easy yes for you?" you learn where their flexibility is — sometimes it's not where you expect.

### 4. Never give a single number when asked first

If asked "what's your expected comp?" — give a range. The bottom of the range is above your target. The top of the range is the aspirational.

Example: "Based on what I'm seeing in the market for roles at this level and geography, the range I'm working with is €X–Y, ideally landing closer to the top of that range. What's the band you're working with on your side?"

### 5. Don't reveal the walk-away

If you say "I'd walk below €X," you've just told them their floor. The walk-away is internal.

If pushed hard for a minimum, deflect: "I'm not anchored on a hard minimum yet — I'd want to see the whole package before committing to a number."

### 6. Counter at least once

Even if the first offer is good, counter at least once. The counter-and-improve dance is expected; accepting on the first offer often leaves money on the table and reads as either inexperienced or under-leveraged.

The exception: an offer that's already at or above your aspirational price. Then accepting is fine — but slept-on, not in the moment.

### 7. Sleep on it

The single most reliable rule. Never say yes on the call. The dopamine of getting an offer is real; the calm read 24 hours later is different. The walk-away tests differently the next morning.

If pressured to decide immediately, that pressure itself is information about the company. A reasonable counterpart gives a reasonable thinking window.

## Pre-negotiation prep checklist

Before any negotiation conversation, you should have:

- [ ] Written walk-away number, with reasoning
- [ ] Written target number, anchored to market data or scope
- [ ] Written aspirational number, for the "what would close this" question
- [ ] BATNA value estimated honestly (not flattered)
- [ ] Top 2–3 levers prioritized
- [ ] Counter scripts for the top 2 levers drafted
- [ ] Decided which levers are "must-have" (e.g., work-from-anywhere in writing) vs. "nice-to-have"
- [ ] One night of sleep between getting the offer and counter-proposing

The skill produces all of these in the output. The night of sleep is on you.

## Anti-patterns

### Personal-needs anchoring

"I have a mortgage" or "I have a family to support" — these turn the conversation into a sob story and weaken your position. The other side may sympathize but won't increase the offer based on personal need.

The anchor is market value, role scope, or your experience — never personal need.

### Apologizing for negotiating

"Sorry to ask, but…" or "I know this might be too much…" — these soften the ask in a way that signals lack of conviction. The skill's voice rules apply here especially: hedging language is filler.

### Threatening to walk

"I'll walk if you can't meet X" is a power play that backfires more often than it works. The walk-away is internal; if you're actually going to walk, you walk — you don't threaten to.

The exception: if asked directly whether the walk-away is real ("we can't do that — is that a dealbreaker?"), then honesty is appropriate ("yes, that's the line for me").

### Counter-then-cave

Counter at €X, then immediately cave to €Y when they push back. The cave signals the counter wasn't real. Better to hold the counter with reasoning, even if it leads to a slower conversation.

### Negotiating in the moment of excitement

The dopamine of getting an offer is real. Negotiating in that state usually leads to accepting more than you should and asking for less than you should. Sleep first.

## Jurisdiction-specific notes

These don't replace a qualified tax advisor — they flag what to check in your own
country of tax residence. (The examples in parentheses are illustrative.)

- **Income tax** is usually progressive, and rates can vary by region. Net comp matters
  more than gross — know your top marginal rate.
- **Self-employed / contractor income** is often taxed separately, with extra
  social-security obligations on top of income tax. If a role pushes you to stop
  consulting, that lost income is a real comp component.
- **Equity** has specific tax treatment depending on whether it's RSU, ISO, NSO, or a
  local equivalent. Vesting and exercise timing matter. Verify before signing.
- **Benefits in kind** (private health, meal vouchers, transit) often have specific tax
  treatment — some advantaged, some not. Worth verifying.
- **Sign-on bonus** is typically fully taxable as ordinary income in the year received.
  No grossing-up unless specifically negotiated.

When the comp comparison involves a foreign employer (e.g. one paying via an
Employer-of-Record), your country-of-residence tax treatment usually still applies
because that's where you're tax-resident.
