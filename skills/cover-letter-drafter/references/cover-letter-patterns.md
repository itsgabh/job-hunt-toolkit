# Cover Letter Patterns

Four opener archetypes, each with structure, two example openings, common pitfalls, and a tone calibration note. Read alongside `voice-and-style.md` — the patterns set the shape, the voice guide sets the tone.

The three-paragraph structure (Opener → Proof → Fit and close) is the same across all four archetypes. What changes is the opener and which thread from your profile leads the proof paragraph. The examples below are anonymized illustrations — the skill draws real specifics from `profile.md` and the JD.

---

## 1. Problem-opener

**When to use:** the JD names a specific challenge the team is trying to solve. Examples: "scaling support as we grow from 100 to 300 customers," "rolling out a new platform across several regions," "standing up the program from scratch."

**Structure:** Name the problem the team faces (one sentence, paraphrasing the JD honestly). Connect it to a specific moment when you solved a structurally similar problem (one sentence). End the paragraph with the bridge into the proof paragraph.

**Example A (multi-region operations, fictional illustration):**
> Keeping a service operation consistent across [several regions] without slowing the team down is a real problem, especially when each region runs on a slightly different mix of in-house and outsourced processes. In a prior role I owned the workflows behind a small team supporting [N] sites across [M] regions — the kind of cross-region lift that only stays clean if the systems are designed for it.

**Example B (strategy-to-execution seam, fictional illustration):**
> Translating high-level strategy into an operational rhythm is the work I'd want to spend the next chapter on. The [Role] sits in exactly that seam — where planning, communication, and delivery have to move as one rather than as three.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Misreading the problem.** If the JD says "scaling the operation" but the actual problem is "the founder is exhausted and needs a senior hire to take everything off their plate," the problem-opener will sound presumptuous. When in doubt, switch to direct-fit.
- **Restating the JD as the problem.** "You're looking for a Senior Specialist to support the regional team" isn't a problem-opener; it's a tautology.
- **Solving the problem in the opener.** The opener names the problem and signals you can address it. The proof paragraph does the actual solving.

**Tone:** confident, not lecturing. The opener is a peer-to-peer framing of the problem, not a consultant pitch.

## 2. Story-opener

**When to use:** a specific experience of yours maps tightly to what the role asks for, and the story has a clean hook in the first sentence (e.g. a systems migration you led, a cross-functional program, a regional rollout).

**Structure:** Open with the concrete moment (one sentence — when, what, scope). Pull out the through-line that connects it to the JD (one sentence). Bridge into the proof paragraph that expands the same story.

**Example A (leading with a systems migration, fictional illustration):**
> Last year I ran a billing-platform cutover — rebuilding the invoicing engine, dunning rules, and revenue reports from scratch for a finance team mid-audit. The work mapped onto exactly the kind of systems implementation the [Company] role is set up around: the configuration craft, the testing discipline, the rollout cadence.

**Example B (leading with an early leadership role, fictional illustration):**
> One of the earliest pieces of work that shaped how I think about operations was a route-planning role across a 12-city delivery network — coordinating dispatch teams and building the dashboards that kept on-time rates steady through peak season. The translation work between strategy and execution at scale has been my favorite kind of problem ever since.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Story bloat.** The opener gets 60–90 words, not 200. The full story belongs in the proof paragraph.
- **Story doesn't connect.** If you have to work hard to bridge the story to the JD, it's the wrong story. Pick another.
- **Over-romanticizing.** Keep the register reflective, not sentimental. "It taught me everything I know about leadership" is too much. "It's where the pattern clicked" is the right register.

**Tone:** reflective, grounded. The story is told as fact, not as a parable.

## 3. Direct-fit-opener

**When to use:** the role-fit alignment is unusually clean — most of the JD's "what we're looking for" maps to your profile directly. Or when no other archetype fits cleanly and you need the lowest-risk option. This is the default.

**Structure:** Name the role and the company. State the alignment in one specific sentence (not "I'm a strong fit" but "[N] years at remote-first companies — including a recent platform migration — lines up with what the role's set up to do"). Bridge into proof.

**Example A (clean fit, fictional illustration):**
> The [Role] role at [Company] is a clean match for the work I've spent the last [N] years building toward. Multi-region process ownership, cross-vendor coordination, mid-market platform work, and an explicit appetite for AI and automation — those are the threads I lead with.

**Example B (fit with an acknowledged gap, fictional illustration):**
> The [Role] role at [Company] sits where I spend most of my time today — systems work, end-to-end ownership across a multi-region team, and an explicit push to bring AI into the function. The match isn't perfect (more on the specific platform stack in a moment), but the shape of the work is.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Generic.** "I am a strong fit for this role" is not a direct-fit opener. Specifics are everything.
- **Overclaiming on the gap.** Example B above acknowledges the specific platform-stack gap in the opener and then explains it in proof. That's the honest move when the gap is visible. Don't hide it; don't lead with it either.
- **Sounding like a credentials dump.** The opener names two or three threads, not all twelve.

**Tone:** matter-of-fact. Direct fit is the opener for when you can let the alignment speak.

## 4. Why-this-company-opener

**When to use:** you have genuine resonance with the company's mission, values, or direction — and can name a specific reason that isn't borrowable. Examples: a non-profit working on something you care about, a remote-first company whose stated operating principles match yours, an AI-forward company doing something specific with AI that connects to your work.

**Structure:** Name the specific thing about the company that pulled you in (one sentence, with evidence — a values page, a founder post, a product detail). Connect it to where you are in your own arc (one sentence). Bridge to proof.

**Example A (category resonance, fictional illustration):**
> [The company's category or core idea] — [a one-line articulation of it] — is exactly the kind of structural problem I've spent my time trying to solve internally for my own team. The cross-functional, async-native approach the [Company] team has written about is the operating model I've been building toward.

**Example B (mission resonance, fictional illustration):**
> The reason I'm writing about the [Role] role at [Org] is the through-line in the work, not the title. After [your earlier chapter] and the last [N] years in remote-first operations at scale, I want the next chapter to be at an organization where the mission is the reason for the function, not the marketing layer over it.

**Pitfalls:**

- **Flattery.** "I am a huge admirer of what you're doing at [Company]" is not a why-this-company opener. Specifics or skip the archetype.
- **Mission-washing.** Don't claim resonance with a mission you don't have. If the company is a standard enterprise SaaS with no real mission angle, don't force this archetype — use direct fit.
- **Sounding sycophantic.** Don't fawn. The opener is "this is the work I want to be doing," not "you are amazing."

**Tone:** reflective and forward-looking. This archetype is the one where your career-direction language (from `profile.md`) can show up.

---

## Cross-archetype rules

- **One archetype per letter.** Don't mix problem-opener with why-this-company in the same opening paragraph. Pick one.
- **Opener length is fixed.** 60–90 words for the opener paragraph regardless of archetype. If it's running longer, the opener is doing the proof paragraph's job.
- **The alternative opener uses a different archetype.** When Step 5 asks for an alternative opener, switch archetypes — not just hooks. Direct-fit + direct-fit with different details is not a meaningful A/B.
- **No archetype is right for every JD.** If none of these four fit cleanly, the cover letter probably shouldn't be written from a JD alone — you need to surface a specific connection (a person, a project, a moment) that turns the letter from generic to specific.
