# Smart Question Archetypes

Question patterns organized by **interview stage** and by **pain point**. The skill draws from these to draft 5–7 questions per brief — the right mix changes with the stage.

A smart question does two things at once: it signals you did the homework, and it tests whether the role is actually right for you. Bad questions are generic ("what's the culture like?") or test-the-interviewer ("describe your management style"). Both waste the question.

---

## By interview stage

### Recruiter screen (typically 30 min)

The recruiter usually can't speak to deep role specifics, but can confirm comp range, structure, timeline, and the "shape" of the team.

**What to ask:**

- "What's the compensation range for this role?"
- "Who is the hiring manager, and what's their team's structure?"
- "What's the rough timeline for the process — and are there other candidates in flight?"
- "What's the work model — fully remote, hybrid? If hybrid, what's the cadence expectation?"
- "Is the work-from-anywhere policy something I should ask about now, or later in the process?" (use only if your profile flags recurring stays abroad)
- "What is the recruiter looking for that they don't usually see on the resumes coming through?" (gold question — invites them to tell you what matters)

**What NOT to ask the recruiter:**

- Strategy questions (they can't answer)
- "Describe the culture" (generic)
- Detailed role-shape questions (save for the hiring manager)
- Aggressive comp negotiation (this is a screen, not the negotiation conversation)

---

### Hiring manager (typically 45–60 min)

The hiring manager owns the role. This is where to dig into scope, success criteria, team dynamics, and what year one actually looks like.

**What to ask:**

- "What does success in year one look like, concretely? What does the person sitting in this seat have done by month 12?"
- "Walk me through the team — who are the people I'd work with day to day, and what are they each good at?"
- "What's the hardest thing about this role that isn't obvious from the JD?"
- "Where does [the function you'd join] sit in the strategic conversations of the company? What's the next-quarter priority you'd want this role to influence?"
- "How do you like to be partnered with? What's the rhythm that works for you?"
- "What's the next hire into this team after this one — or is this it for the year?"
- "If I asked the most senior IC on your current team what makes a good day, what would they say?"

**What NOT to ask the hiring manager:**

- Comp specifics (already covered with recruiter; can reopen later)
- "What's your management style?" (turns it into them performing for you)
- "Why did the last person leave?" (technically fine but easy to misframe — better: "What's changed about the role since the last person sat in it?")

---

### Panel / cross-functional (typically 30 min each)

Panel rounds are where you meet peers from other functions — typically a hiring manager from another team, sometimes a peer from the function you'd join, sometimes a stakeholder you'd partner with closely.

**What to ask each panelist:**

- "What does [the function you'd join] get right today, from where you sit? And what would you want it to get better at?"
- "What's the most painful process friction with [the function you'd join] your team currently feels?"
- "How does cross-functional decision-making work here? Who do I need to know if I want to ship something that touches your team?"
- "What does your team look like in a year?" (gives you organizational direction signal)

**What NOT to ask:**

- Questions only the hiring manager can answer (don't waste cross-functional time on scope clarification)
- Generic "what's the culture" — easy to ask, low information

---

### Final / exec round (typically 30 min with a CEO, CPO, COO, or founder)

This is where to ask about direction, conviction, and what the company is *actually* trying to build — not what's on the careers page.

**What to ask:**

- "What's the conviction behind [the function you'd join] existing at the size and shape it does today?"
- "If [Company] is twice as big in two years, what does [the function you'd join] look like — and what does this role's seat become?"
- "What's the thing about the company you'd warn me about?"
- "What's a recent decision the leadership team made that you're proud of — and one you'd revisit?"
- "What do you want from a partner in this function that you don't currently have?"

**What NOT to ask:**

- Tactical questions (they're not in the role detail)
- "Why this role?" reversed (asking why the role exists is fine; asking why they're hiring is too easy to misread as challenge)
- Anything that signals you haven't done the homework on the company

## By pain point

When the brief identifies a specific pain point, the smart question should connect to it directly. Some patterns:

### "Formalizing the function" (often at Early-stage companies)

- "What's the part of the work currently held in someone's head that you most want documented?"
- "Where's the function today vs. where you want it in 12 months?"
- "What's the trigger for hiring the next role into this team after this one?"

### "Rolling out [a major platform in the field]"

- "Where are you in the rollout lifecycle? What phase comes next?"
- "What's the change-management cadence around the rollout? How are you communicating it to employees?"
- "What's the trade-off you've made between configuration breadth and rollout speed?"

### "Recent layoffs"

- "How is the team thinking about retention coming out of the last six months?"
- "What's changed about how [the function you'd join] is operating since the reduction?"
- "What does morale look like, honestly, from where you sit?"

### "Rapid expansion (regional or scale)"

- "Where's the most operational friction in the current scaling?"
- "What's the biggest process in this function you'd want fixed before you double again?"
- "How does EMEA / APAC / [region] coordinate with central programs?"

### "AI / automation focus"

- "What's your appetite for ICs bringing AI into their workflow without explicit approval? Or is there a more structured rollout you're running?"
- "What's the bar for an AI-assisted process replacing a manual one?"
- "Where would you not want AI applied?" (good for surfacing AI maturity vs. AI buzz)

### "Founder-led function transitioning"

- "What work in this area is currently sitting with the founder or COO that you want to lift off?"
- "What's the founder's posture going to be once this hire is in seat? Hands-off, partnered, or still owning specific calls?"

## Question construction principles

- **Specific over generic.** Always.
- **Open-ended over yes / no.** A yes / no question wastes the airtime.
- **Tied to the brief.** Reference something specific you found in research. "I saw that [Company] just opened up two new EMEA offices — how is [the function you'd join] organizing around that?" beats "How do you handle multi-country operations?"
- **No more than 5–7 in total across the round.** Pick the ones that earn their place.
- **Order matters.** Lead with the question that signals you've done the homework; save the riskier or more direct questions (the "what would you warn me about" type) for later in the round when rapport is built.
- **Listen for follow-ups.** The best question is sometimes the one that comes from what they just said. The list is a backstop, not a script.

---

## What "good" looks like in a Smart Questions section

- Each question is one or two sentences, max
- Each question references something specific (a system, a recent move, a named priority)
- The mix is calibrated to the stage — recruiter screen has different questions than hiring manager
- 5–7 total, with 1–2 marked as "optional / if time"

## What "bad" looks like

- "What's the culture like?"
- "How would you describe your management style?"
- The same question that would work for any company at any stage
- A list of 12 questions (signals overprep, you'll never use them all)
