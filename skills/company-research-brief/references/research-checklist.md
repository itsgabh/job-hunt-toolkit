# Research Checklist

What to search for, in what order, and what "good" looks like per section. Use this as the working pass-through during a research run — read top to bottom, capture as you go.

---

## Section 1: Company snapshot

**What to search:**

- `"[company] overview"` or `"[company] about"`
- `"[company] business model"`
- `"[company] funding"` or `"[company] crunchbase"`
- `"[company] employees"` (LinkedIn People page is usually the most accurate)

**Primary sources (in order of preference):**

1. Company's own About / Company page
2. Founder's LinkedIn or About-the-founder page
3. Crunchbase (for funding)
4. Company's LinkedIn (for headcount and geographic distribution)
5. Wikipedia (use with caution — often outdated)

**What good looks like:**

- Concrete stage (Seed / Series A / B / C / D / Late stage / Public)
- Total raised + latest round date
- Current headcount with source date
- Geographic distribution (HQ + key offices + remote workforce note)
- Business model in one sentence (who pays them, for what)

**What bad looks like:**

- "Leading provider of…" framing copied from the company's marketing page
- Headcount without a source (LinkedIn People page count is fine; "around 200" with no source is not)
- Conflating "raised" with "valuation"

---

## Section 2: Recent moves (last 12 months)

**What to search:**

- `"[company] news 2026"` (or current year)
- `"[company] layoffs"`
- `"[company] CEO"` (catches leadership changes)
- `"[company] launches"` or `"[company] announces"`
- `"[company] series"` (catches recent funding)
- `"[company] acquires"` (catches acquisitions)

**Primary sources:**

1. Company blog and press releases
2. TechCrunch, The Information, Sifted (EU), Axios / Business Insider (US / global), Crunchbase News
3. Founder LinkedIn / X posts
4. PitchBook / Crunchbase (for funding events specifically)

**Date discipline:**

- **Only include items from the last 12 months.** Older signals are usually weaker than current ones.
- **Date every claim explicitly.** "Funded in March 2025" beats "recently funded."
- **Flag stale data.** If the last signal you can find is from 18 months ago, say so — the absence is itself a signal.

**What good looks like:**

- A small set (3–8) of dated, sourced moves
- Each move named at the right level of detail ("raised $40M Series B led by Accel" not just "raised funding")
- Mix of types (funding, leadership, product, market) where available

**What bad looks like:**

- Five entries that are all variants of "released a feature" from the company blog
- Undated claims
- Moves from 2023 with no recency check

## Section 3: Function signals

(Signals about the function the role sits in — substitute the relevant titles and keywords for the field. The example searches below use a People/HR role; swap them for the equivalents in Finance, Engineering, Marketing, etc.)

**What to search:**

- `"[company] head of [function]"` or `"[company] VP [function]"` or the C-level title for the field [example, People: "head of people," "VP people," "CHRO"]
- `"[company] careers"` (look at how many open roles in the function exist and what categories)
- `"[company] glassdoor"` (use with care — noisy, but spikes in negative reviews are signal)
- `"[company] hiring philosophy"` or `"[company] culture"`
- LinkedIn search: people at [company] with titles matching the function [example, People: "People," "Talent," "HR," "Recruiting"]

**Primary sources:**

1. LinkedIn — for who's on the team, their backgrounds, and recent hires
2. Company careers page — for currently open roles in the function
3. Company blog (if they write about the function's work — async-first companies sometimes do)
4. Podcast appearances by the function's leader (rich source if available)
5. Glassdoor / RepVue (skim for patterns, ignore single-incident venting)

**What good looks like:**

- Named leader of the function with background and tenure
- A count of the function's staff visible on LinkedIn (size of function)
- A read of what else is open (e.g., "they also have two adjacent roles open" signals the function is being built out)
- One or two specific signals about hiring philosophy or culture from a primary source

**What bad looks like:**

- "Strong culture, employees are happy" with no sourcing
- Confidently naming a function leader without verifying tenure (people leave)
- Ignoring Glassdoor entirely when there are clear patterns

## Section 4: Cultural and values signals

**What to search:**

- `"[company] values"`
- `"[company] culture deck"` or `"[company] handbook"`
- `"[founder name] blog"` or `"[founder name] interview"`
- `"[company] async"` or `"[company] remote"` (if relevant to the role)
- `"[company] work from anywhere"` (relevant if your profile flags recurring stays abroad)

**Primary sources:**

1. Company's own values page or handbook (if public — some companies publish theirs)
2. Founder posts on LinkedIn / X / personal blog
3. Podcast appearances by leadership
4. About / Careers page culture language

**What good looks like:**

- Direct quotes from primary sources, with attribution
- A read on whether the values language matches the operating model (companies whose careers page says "async-first" but whose calendar invites all hit 9am PT are not actually async-first)
- Specific flexibility signals — explicit work-from-anywhere policies, PTO norms, hours flexibility, hybrid cadence

**What bad looks like:**

- Generic culture summaries
- Mistaking marketing values copy for actual values
- Ignoring the flexibility specifics (these often matter most — check your profile)

---

## Section 5: Industry and competitive context

**What to search:**

- `"[company] vs"` (catches competitor comparisons)
- `"[company] category"` or `"[industry] companies"`
- Industry analyst coverage if relevant (Gartner, Forrester for B2B SaaS)

**Primary sources:**

1. Industry analyst reports (often paywalled but summaries are findable)
2. Crunchbase competitor lists
3. TechCrunch / Sifted (EU) / US business-press analysis pieces
4. Founder posts about category positioning

**What good looks like:**

- 2–3 named competitors
- One sentence on where this company sits in the category
- A read on market timing (early in category, mature, consolidating)

**Skip this section if:** the role doesn't require commercial / business-context judgment. For a narrowly-scoped specialist role ([your target role]), market context matters less than it would for a strategy-adjacent role.

---

## Time-boxing the search

**Target:** 8–12 search calls across all five sections.

**Sign you're spinning:**

- More than 20 calls
- Repeating queries with minor variations
- Going deeper on a section that already has enough signal

**Sign you're under-searching:**

- Fewer than five distinct sources cited
- No primary sources in the function-signals section
- No specific date on any "recent move"

When time-boxed, prioritize: snapshot > recent moves > function signals > culture > industry.
