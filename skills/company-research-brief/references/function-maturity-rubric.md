# Function Maturity Rubric

How to read where a company's **function you'd join** sits — and what that means for the role you're looking at. This works for any function: Finance, Engineering, Marketing, Operations, People, and so on. Maturity is a judgment, not a search result; this rubric is the reasoning framework, not a checklist.

Four levels: **Founder-led / Early / Mid / Mature**. Each has defining signals, typical roles, day-job texture, risks for someone at your level joining, and implications for the smart questions to ask.

Throughout, an HR/People example is given in brackets as *one* illustration. Substitute the equivalents from whatever field the role sits in (the systems, the leadership titles, the sub-specialties).

---

## Level 1: Founder-led / no formal function

**Defining signals:**

- Headcount typically under 30–50
- No dedicated leader for the function; a founder or generalist executive owns it
- The work is mixed into ops or admin
- External vendors or contractors handle the specialized parts [example, People: an EOR like Remote or Deel handles compliance]
- No openings for the function besides the one you're considering (or just one)

**Typical roles open at this level:**

- "First hire" for the function / functional lead — often broadly scoped
- Frequently a hybrid title (function + ops, function + an adjacent area)

**Day-job texture:**

- Greenfield — almost nothing exists; the person hired builds the function
- Wide scope — multiple sub-areas all land on one plate
- Reports to a founder, not to another functional leader
- Closer to founder than typical (high leverage, high context-switching)

**Risks to weigh for your level:**

- Role definition unclear; scope can shift weekly
- No peer in the function to learn from
- Standards and tooling probably ad hoc — defining them is part of the job
- High visibility but high context cost

**Smart questions to ask at this level:**

- "What does success in year one look like, in terms of what exists by the end of it?"
- "Who's currently making these decisions, and how does that shift when I join?"
- "What's the budget posture for hiring into this function? Is this a one-hire investment or the start of a function?"

## Level 2: Early function

**Defining signals:**

- Headcount roughly 30–150
- 1–3 people in the function (a functional manager plus maybe one specialist)
- Core systems are in place but probably mid-market [example, People: an HRIS like HiBob or Personio, an ATS like Greenhouse or Ashby]
- Some specialization but most of the team are generalists
- A small handful of openings in the function

**Typical roles open at this level:**

- Specialist / Senior Specialist in the function
- A partner / business-partner role at a low ratio (one partner for the whole company)
- A first dedicated hire in a sub-area

**Day-job texture:**

- Function exists but is skeletal — programs are being built, not maintained
- Heavy execution work alongside some program design
- One step removed from the founder; reports into a functional lead or a COO
- Cross-functional partnership is constant (with eng, finance, legal)

**Risks to weigh for your level:**

- Probably "Senior IC" by title but with manager-level scope expectations
- Often a single point of failure (sole owner of a key system or process)
- Promotion path can be vague — the lead role is likely already filled

**Smart questions to ask at this level:**

- "Walk me through how the team is structured today. Where does this role sit, and what's the reporting line?"
- "What does the team look like a year from now? Are you planning to grow the function alongside the company, or stay lean?"
- "What's the most painful unsolved problem in the function right now?"

## Level 3: Mid function

**Defining signals:**

- Headcount roughly 150–600
- 5–15 people in the function
- Named sub-specialties exist [example, People: a Talent Acquisition lead, an Ops lead, an L&D / Engagement lead, sometimes an Analytics IC]
- Core systems are in place and possibly enterprise-grade at the top of this range
- Multiple tools, often partially integrated
- A senior leader for the function exists — Head of, VP, or a chief
- Multiple roles in the function open at any time

**Typical roles open at this level:**

- Senior Specialist (often regional — "EMEA," "APAC")
- A partner role with named function alignment (e.g., aligned to Engineering)
- Sub-area manager or specialist roles
- A team-lead role (with people management responsibility)

**Day-job texture:**

- Function is real — programs exist and are being optimized rather than built
- Heavier specialization; less generalist scope
- Multiple peers; learning-from-peers is part of the value
- Senior leadership above the role; less direct founder access

**Risks to weigh for your level:**

- Scope can feel narrower than the founder-led or early roles
- More political / coordination overhead
- Promotion paths are clearer but slower
- Tooling can include enterprise platforms you may not have used

**Smart questions to ask at this level:**

- "How is the function organized? Where does this role sit in that structure?"
- "What's the relationship between regional teams and global / central programs?"
- "Where are you in the lifecycle of [a major build or rollout in the field]? What does the next 6 months look like on that?"

---

## Level 4: Mature function

**Defining signals:**

- Headcount typically 600+
- 15+ people in the function across multiple specialties
- Distinct sub-teams / centers of excellence [example, People: TA, People Ops, Comp & Ben, L&D, DEI, People Analytics]
- Enterprise tooling, often with global / regional splits
- A C-level leader for the function; it reports to the CEO

**Typical roles open at this level:**

- Senior Specialist (deep specialization)
- Specialist roles in narrow domains
- Manager / Senior Manager roles in named pillars

**Day-job texture:**

- Programs are mature and being optimized at the margin
- High specialization; the IC's scope is narrow but deep
- Lots of stakeholders (regional partners, function partners, central program owners)
- Process discipline is high; ambiguity is low

**Risks to weigh for your level:**

- Scope may be narrower than your current role (check against your profile)
- More process, less greenfield
- Career-direction-wise, this is "lateral or sideways" unless the role explicitly stretches scope
- A named enterprise-platform gap will bite if that platform is a key project

**Smart questions to ask at this level:**

- "What's the autonomy posture for ICs in this role? How much of the work is following central programs vs. shaping them?"
- "Where does this function sit in the strategic conversations of the company?"
- "What's the function's appetite for new tools or process changes brought up by ICs?"

---

## Assigning a maturity level

Use the defining signals as the primary input, but state the reasoning in the brief. If the signals point to two levels (e.g., headcount says Mid but staffing says Early), name the ambiguity — that's often itself a signal about the company's growth phase.

**When in doubt, the level you assign should pass this test:** can you read the level + the reasoning and understand what kind of job you'd be walking into? If the answer is "not really, the brief just labeled it," reassess.

---

## Your fit by level (rough heuristic — calibrate to your profile)

- **Founder-led:** Strong fit if the role offers real leadership scope (a functional lead), especially at mission-driven orgs. Risk: founder dynamics, isolation.
- **Early:** Often a strong fit for an experienced IC — much hands-on, build-from-scratch work. Calibrate to your own profile.
- **Mid:** Good fit for senior IC roles, especially with regional scope. Watch for named-tool gaps (e.g. an enterprise platform you haven't used) and scope narrower than your current autonomy.
- **Mature:** Lateral at best. Worth applying only when the role explicitly stretches scope or comes with formal management.
